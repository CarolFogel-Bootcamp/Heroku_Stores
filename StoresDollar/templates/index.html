Unaffordable Access
Dollar Stores and the American Food Desert

Low Price, High Cost

In 2018, a number of industry publications and media sources reported that the growth of dollar stores is driving grocery stores out of business and perpetuating food deserts. City centers and rural areas in which residents already lack access to healthy food are targets for dollar stores.

“Today, there are more dollar stores in the United States than all Walmarts and Starbucks combined. These low-priced “small-box” retailers, like Dollar General, offer little to no fresh food—yet they feed more Americans than either Trader Joe’s or Whole Foods, and are gaining on the country’s largest food retailers.” Civil Eats

To test the hypothesis that dollar stores are rising predominantly in food deserts and are driving out grocery stores, our team analyzed geographic and longitudinal data from the US Census Bureau and the USDA.

Analysis

Today, there are at least 23.5 million Americans living in food deserts, low-income areas that lack easy access to grocery stores.1 In many low access communities, grocery stores are either too far away to allow for regular purchase of perishable fresh food or are located in neighborhoods that are not easy to access by walking or public transportation. Dollar stores fill in the distance gap in these rural and inner city communities by occupying smaller stores that are closer to low income populations. While dollar stores advertise convenience and affordability, the majority of their food offerings are highly processed items that are low in nutritional value. This makes shopping choices harder for low income families with limited access to transportation.


Food Desert by County

Map of USDA data shows the percentage of county population identified as having low access to food. The largest tracts of low food access are in the Mountain Region. Counties with the highest numbers of food desert tracts are in the South and Midwest.



Dollar Store Locations

On a national level, dollar stores are more densely located on the Eastern half of the United States. This could be due to population density. Another potential factor is that the Dollar General and the Family Dollar headquarters are located in Tennessee and North Carolina.


By the Numbers

Dollar stores – namely Dollar General, Dollar Tree, and Family Dollar – comprise one of the fastest-growing retailers in the U.S. “Dollar General stores went from about 8,828 to 15,370 in the last 10 years, totaling a 74% increase. Meanwhile, net income more than quadrupled to $1.59 billion.” Hutch News

Predictions

Machine Learning

In order to see the effects of these businesses on people, all the input data was linked to population. Since the number of residents vary greatly by the geographic size and density of each county, the training data was normalized by calculating the average number of residents serviced per store.

Since the USDA only releases food access data every five years, 2010 and 2015, machine learning was utilized to fill in the gap years. For every year from 2005 to 2016, the number of people with low access to food in each county was modeled on the average number of customers per stores, employee count and employee compensation.

People and Patterns

When we used population as a common denominator between these retailers and people, patterns show that grocery stores and dollar stores target different populations. The grocery store scatter plot shows little connection between number of stores and levels of access. A county can have a lot of stores and still a high percentage of people with limited access to healthy food. This leads us to believe that there are other contributing factors not accounted for such as income, the neighborhoods the stores are situated in and whether adequate transportation is available. The dollar store scatter plot reveals that dollar store tends to concentrate on urban and rural areas in which the 20 to 35 percent of the population lacks access to food.

Conclusion

EFFECTS ON PEOPLE

Through visualizing the connections between grocery stores, dollar stores and food deserts, it is clear to see that people who have limited choices will not benefit from the growth of dollar stores. While some counties are seeing an uptick in grocery stores, the areas that are most at risk are seeing grocery stores stagnate or close while dollar stores are expanding. Dollar stores recognize customer demand for food products, but the future for people living with low access is bleak as plans to provide nutritional items are up to the stores’ discretions.

Methodology

One of the biggest challenges facing our team is how to parse and filter the data to tell an accurate story. The level of details and selection of time heavily influence the visibility of impact.

Finding comparable information means matching data that either is lacking in geographical or longitudinal data. One set of information had geographic data down to longitude and latitude, but lacked a time factor. To gather dollar store information over time, we used NAICS codes for businesses categorized at Other General Merchandise. While this category has the dollar store corporations as its top businesses, it also contains some false data for other types of businesses. However, to check for correlations and links between business patterns and livelihood of people, we believe that this data is an appropriate proxy. For more in depth observations, we would factor income, transportation and health information.
